3. DESIGN RATIONALE

The iPal is novel in that it supports all three major personal assistant platforms, complementing Siri devices to a degree yet to be seen in the current market. We view flexibility as a priority; changes in Amazon or Google Home's platforms may require modifications of our own. The sections below discuss our module design choices and their respective advantages.

3.1 Incorporating Siri, Amazon, and Google

Since its incorporation into the iPhone, Siri has become an area of tremendous focus for the hands-free software development community. Apple's release of SiriKit has further facilitated the adoption of Siri by existing software companies. Although Apple has limited Siri-centric development to only a handful of application functionality domains, it is quite likely that this selection will see great expansion in the near future. Ultimately, this will translate to a higher number of a user's applications supporting Siri-involved, Siri-enhanced, and even Siri-based operations.

The growth of Siri has not been unnoticed by rival software companies. Apple and Google have both developed their own sophisticated hands-free platforms, however, they currently remain proprietary. Our aim was to combine the best of all three – Amazon's practical online shopping platform; Google Home's precise management features for the constantly changing internet of things in the modern-day home; the familiarity of Siri – its pragmatic usability, and its overwhelming support for today's most popular applications.

Since the iPal is, in essence, an extension of Siri combined with the most important features of its competitors, it will enable a greater degree of functionality than each of its competitors alone.

3.2 Security

Security was another priority in our design of the iPal. Authentication is necessary for sensitive operations, such as purchases or message retrieval. Current hands-free assistant devices lack robust authentication models. The Amazon Echo presents users with the option to create a password, but requires them to repeat it out loud each time they are prompted to authenticate, making it susceptible to theft. While Google has patented a voice recognition algorithm, they have yet to implement it in any of their hands-free assistant platforms. Both Siri and Google operate on the assumption that the current user is the rightful device owner.

Our design includes a two-factor authentication scheme based on user identify and voice recognition. We believe this is near impossible to subvert as it hinges on identifying key voice "features", such as pitch and frequency.

Additionally, we aimed to allow users freedom in setting security parameters as they choose. Rather than restricting them to a static security hierarchy, we will give them the ability to set custom user permissions at their discretion.

3.3 Speech Processing Module

Our Speech Processing Module is the iPal's initial contact point with users, acting as both the command receiver and primary coordinator for the resulting data flow. We recognize the importance of differentiating between Siri, Amazon, and Google Home commands, and constructed our resulting module with the ability to carry out this process before proceeding.

3.4 Machine-Learning Module

Since we cannot benefit from an Amazon or Google Home API, we sought to recreate the machine-learning features of Siri's Web Services in extending support to the two platforms. Rather than making use of a single machine-learning algorithm, we opted for three; this allows us to determine inquiry-answer correlation with superior precision, and in conjunction training on stored answers, provide a more optimized user experience.

We maintain flexibility by supporting the incorporation of additional machine-learning algorithms.

3.5 Interoperability Module

The Interoperability Module is the sole point of communication with Amazon's and Google Home's web services. We believe that our design will allow this module to accommodate any change Amazon or Google Home make in the future.  

To facilitate easy changeability, we adopted a high-level of abstraction in designing our Amazon and Google Home interfaces as two separate modules. Should either of these change their service platform, we could swiftly adapt the corresponding module with minimal impact.    

The WebService class is responsible for identifying the inquiry format and required protocol. Our design allows for easy differentiation between JSON and RESTful types, as well as HTTP and TCP protocols.
